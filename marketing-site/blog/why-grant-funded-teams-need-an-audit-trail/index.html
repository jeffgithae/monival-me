Why grant-funded teams need an audit trail, not just a report

June 2026 · Evidara team

A clean, well-formatted donor report tells a funder what happened. It doesn't tell them how you know it's true. That gap is exactly where compliance reviews and audits spend most of their time — and it's the part most NGO reporting processes handle the worst.

What a reviewer actually asks

When an auditor or donor compliance officer looks behind a reported number, the question isn't "is this plausible?" — it's "can you show me where this came from, who approved it, and when?" A spreadsheet rarely has a good answer. A figure typed into a cell carries no history. If three people touched the file before the report went out, there is usually no record of what changed, when, or why.

Three things an audit trail should answer

  • Who entered this data, and when? Every activity, indicator result, or budget change should be tied to a specific person and timestamp.
  • Who approved it? A figure that reached a donor report should have passed through a defined review step — not just been typed and trusted.
  • What changed after the fact? If a number was revised, the original value and the reason for the change should still be visible.

Why this matters more as you scale

A three-person pilot project can usually reconstruct what happened from memory and a few emails if a donor asks a pointed question. A program running across multiple grants, multiple field offices, and multiple reporting periods cannot. The bigger the portfolio, the more an audit trail stops being a nice-to-have and starts being the only realistic way to answer a hard question quickly.

What this looks like in practice

In Evidara, every activity, indicator result, and budget change is logged with who made it and when, and routed through an approval workflow before it counts toward a reported figure. When a donor or auditor asks "how do you know this number is correct," the answer is a record, not a recollection.
